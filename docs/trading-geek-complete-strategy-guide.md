# Trading Geek Strategy: Complete Learner Guide

This guide explains the Trading Geek / market-structure strategy in plain English for people learning the model from scratch.

The strategy is not based on indicators calling buy or sell. It is based on reading market structure, liquidity, supply and demand, premium and discount, and waiting for price to confirm a setup in the correct order.

The goal is to find high-quality buy or sell entry areas, not to predict every candle.

## 1. Core Idea

The market moves from liquidity to liquidity.

Retail traders often place stop losses above obvious highs and below obvious lows. The strategy waits for price to take that liquidity, show a real structure shift, and then return to the zone that caused the shift.

A valid setup normally follows this sequence:

1. Find the market direction or active range.
2. Mark premium and discount.
3. Identify meaningful supply or demand.
4. Wait for price to pull back into the correct area.
5. Wait for a liquidity sweep.
6. Wait for Market Shift / Break of Structure.
7. Mark the supply or demand zone that caused the shift.
8. Wait for price to return to that zone.
9. Enter from the zone edge, not randomly in the middle.
10. Place the stop beyond the zone or swept liquidity.
11. Target 3R minimum or the next weak structure / opposing zone if it gives clean room.

The most important lesson is order. A setup is not valid because one thing appears. It is valid only when the whole story checks out in the correct order.

## 2. Strategy Modes

There are two related models. Do not mix them.

### Mode A: Simplified M15 Model

This model uses one main execution timeframe, usually 15 minutes.

Use it when you want a cleaner, simpler day-trading workflow.

Steps:

1. Read M15 structure.
2. Decide whether the market is bullish, bearish, or unclear.
3. Mark the active swing high and swing low.
4. Draw premium and discount on that range.
5. In an uptrend, look for buys in discount.
6. In a downtrend, look for sells in premium.
7. Wait for liquidity to be swept.
8. Wait for BOS / Market Shift after the sweep.
9. Mark the M15 supply or demand zone that caused the shift.
10. Enter at the zone edge when price returns.
11. Stop beyond the zone or sweep.
12. Target 3R or the next weak structure / opposing zone.

This model does not require forcing H4/H1 POI rules into every trade. It focuses on M15 structure, range location, liquidity, BOS, and pullback.

### Mode B: Multi-Timeframe Sniper Model

This model uses multiple timeframes.

Typical layers:

- Daily or H4: main structure and narrative.
- H4 or H1: internal structure and POIs.
- M15, M5, or M1: entry confirmation and refinement.

The rule is:

> A run on the higher timeframe can look like a full trend on the lower timeframe.

So do not confuse a lower-timeframe pullback with a full higher-timeframe reversal.

Steps:

1. Build the higher-timeframe narrative first.
2. Identify strong highs, strong lows, weak highs, and weak lows.
3. Mark higher-timeframe supply and demand POIs.
4. Wait for price to pull back into the correct HTF POI.
5. Drop to the lower timeframe only after price reaches the relevant area.
6. Wait for liquidity sweep.
7. Wait for lower-timeframe Market Shift / BOS.
8. Mark the refined LTF supply or demand zone that caused the shift.
9. Enter at the refined zone edge.
10. Stop beyond the refined zone or sweep.
11. Target weak structure, opposing zone, or minimum 3R.

Lower timeframes refine entries. They do not create independent trades against the higher-timeframe story.

## 3. Market Structure

Market structure tells you what the market is doing.

### Bullish Structure

Bullish structure means price is making:

- Higher highs.
- Higher lows.

In bullish structure, the market is generally expected to take weak highs.

The preferred trade direction is buy, but not at any price. You wait for a pullback into discount or demand.

### Bearish Structure

Bearish structure means price is making:

- Lower highs.
- Lower lows.

In bearish structure, the market is generally expected to take weak lows.

The preferred trade direction is sell, but not at any price. You wait for a pullback into premium or supply.

### Neutral Or Unclear Structure

If structure is mixed, choppy, or unclear, do not force trades.

No-trade is a valid decision.

## 4. Strong And Weak Highs/Lows

This is one of the most important ideas.

### Strong Low

A strong low is a low that caused a bullish break of structure.

It is important because buyers showed enough control from that low to break structure upward.

In bullish conditions, price may return toward a strong low or demand area before continuing higher.

### Weak High

A weak high is a high expected to be taken in bullish order flow.

If the market is bullish, highs above price are often liquidity targets.

### Strong High

A strong high is a high that caused a bearish break of structure.

It is important because sellers showed enough control from that high to break structure downward.

In bearish conditions, price may return toward a strong high or supply area before continuing lower.

### Weak Low

A weak low is a low expected to be taken in bearish order flow.

If the market is bearish, lows below price are often liquidity targets.

### Practical Rule

Trade from strong structure toward weak structure.

- Buy from strong lows / demand and target weak highs.
- Sell from strong highs / supply and target weak lows.

## 5. Active Range And Active Story

Do not use every old candle on the chart for current entries.

First find the important high and low that define the current story. Then identify which anchor came first from left to right.

The active story starts from that first anchor.

Candles before the active story are old context. They can help with direction, but they should not be used to choose fresh entry zones for the current trade.

The strategy focuses on the current active range:

- Bullish: strong low to weak high.
- Bearish: strong high to weak low.
- Range: range low to range high.

## 6. Premium And Discount

Premium and discount tell you where price is expensive or cheap inside the current range.

Draw the range from the active swing low to active swing high.

### Discount

Discount is the lower part of the range.

In bullish conditions, buys are preferred in discount.

Better buys usually appear deeper in discount, not near the middle.

### Premium

Premium is the upper part of the range.

In bearish conditions, sells are preferred in premium.

Better sells usually appear higher in premium, not near the middle.

### Equilibrium

Equilibrium is the middle of the range, around 50%.

This is usually not the best place to enter. It is often noisy and less favorable for risk-to-reward.

### Practical Rule

- Buy in discount.
- Sell in premium.
- Avoid the middle unless there is very strong extra evidence.

For stricter filtering:

- Buys should ideally be in the lower 40-45% of the active range.
- Sells should ideally be in the upper 55-60% of the active range.

In a consolidation:

- Focus on the bottom 30% for demand/buys.
- Focus on the top 30% for supply/sells.
- Avoid the middle 40%.

## 7. Liquidity

Liquidity is where orders and stops are likely sitting.

The market often moves toward obvious liquidity before reversing or continuing.

Common liquidity pools:

- Previous swing highs.
- Previous swing lows.
- Equal highs.
- Equal lows.
- Double tops.
- Double bottoms.
- Trendline liquidity.
- Range highs.
- Range lows.
- Session highs and lows.
- Obvious support and resistance levels.

### Buy-Side Liquidity

Buy-side liquidity sits above highs.

It includes buy stops from breakout traders and stop losses from sellers.

A sweep of buy-side liquidity can prepare a sell setup if followed by bearish shift.

### Sell-Side Liquidity

Sell-side liquidity sits below lows.

It includes sell stops from breakout traders and stop losses from buyers.

A sweep of sell-side liquidity can prepare a buy setup if followed by bullish shift.

## 8. Liquidity Sweep

A liquidity sweep happens when price takes a high or low and then fails to continue.

### Buy-Side Sweep

Price trades above a previous high, takes liquidity, then rejects or closes back below.

This can prepare a sell setup.

But the sweep alone is not enough.

### Sell-Side Sweep

Price trades below a previous low, takes liquidity, then rejects or closes back above.

This can prepare a buy setup.

But the sweep alone is not enough.

### Important Rule

The sweep is the warning.

The Market Shift / BOS is the confirmation.

Do not enter only because liquidity was swept.

## 9. Break Of Structure / Market Shift

After liquidity is swept, price must show that control has shifted.

This is called BOS, CHOCH, or Market Shift depending on context.

### Bullish Shift

After sell-side liquidity is swept, price breaks a meaningful swing high.

This supports a buy setup.

### Bearish Shift

After buy-side liquidity is swept, price breaks a meaningful swing low.

This supports a sell setup.

### Quality Of The Break

A strong break should:

- Break a meaningful level, not tiny noise.
- Close beyond the level.
- Show displacement.
- Leave behind a clear origin zone.

A weak break:

- Barely closes beyond structure.
- Breaks only tiny internal noise.
- Happens in a choppy range.
- Has no displacement.
- Does not create a clean entry zone.

## 10. Displacement

Displacement means price moves strongly away from a zone.

It shows urgency and imbalance.

Good displacement often has:

- Large bodies.
- Directional candles.
- Small pullbacks during the move.
- Clear break of structure.
- Fair value gaps or imbalance.

Displacement helps confirm that the zone that caused the move matters.

But displacement alone is not a complete setup. It must appear in the right story: sweep, shift, pullback, and correct range location.

## 11. Supply And Demand Zones

Supply and demand zones are areas where price previously showed control.

### Demand Zone

A demand zone is an area where buyers took control and pushed price upward.

Common forms:

- Drop-base-rally.
- Rally-base-rally.
- Last bearish candle before bullish displacement.
- Compact bearish/base cluster before bullish BOS.

Use demand for buy setups only when it fits the broader story.

### Supply Zone

A supply zone is an area where sellers took control and pushed price downward.

Common forms:

- Rally-base-drop.
- Drop-base-drop.
- Last bullish candle before bearish displacement.
- Compact bullish/base cluster before bearish BOS.

Use supply for sell setups only when it fits the broader story.

### Good Zone Qualities

A good zone usually:

- Is a compact 1-3 candle base.
- Caused displacement.
- Caused BOS / Market Shift.
- Swept liquidity before moving.
- Is near an extreme of the active range.
- Is fresh or lightly tested.
- Caused the opposing zone to fail.
- Has clean space to target.

### Bad Zone Qualities

A weak zone often:

- Is in the middle of the range.
- Is very wide.
- Is part of choppy price action.
- Has been mitigated many times.
- Did not cause displacement.
- Did not cause BOS.
- Is just a random opposite candle.
- Is approached through slow compression.

## 12. Order Blocks

An order block is a refined candle-level zone inside a supply or demand area.

Do not mark every candle as an order block.

### Bullish Order Block

Usually the last bearish candle or bearish cluster before bullish displacement.

It is valid only if it belongs to a meaningful demand base or caused a bullish shift.

### Bearish Order Block

Usually the last bullish candle or bullish cluster before bearish displacement.

It is valid only if it belongs to a meaningful supply base or caused a bearish shift.

### Order Block Rule

The order block must be tied to at least one of these:

- Liquidity sweep.
- BOS / Market Shift.
- Displacement.
- Higher-timeframe POI.
- Opposing zone failure.
- Active range extreme.

## 13. Fair Value Gap / Imbalance

A fair value gap is an inefficient move where price leaves imbalance.

It can be useful as a pullback area, but it is not a full strategy by itself.

Use FVG as supporting evidence when:

- It appears during displacement.
- It aligns with direction.
- It overlaps supply or demand.
- It is near the entry zone.
- It supports the pullback area.

Avoid using FVG alone as a trade signal.

## 14. Higher-Timeframe POIs

In the sniper model, the higher timeframe matters first.

HTF POIs include:

- H4 supply.
- H4 demand.
- Daily supply.
- Daily demand.
- HTF order blocks.
- Major range extremes.
- Major support/resistance.

The preferred workflow:

1. Identify HTF direction.
2. Mark HTF POIs.
3. Wait for price to pull back into a valid HTF POI.
4. Only then drop to M15/M5/M1 for entry confirmation.

Do not search for lower-timeframe entries in random areas away from the HTF story.

## 15. Zone Ladder

Sometimes price breaks through the first zone.

The strategy should monitor a ladder of zones instead of assuming the first zone must hold.

Zone states:

- Untouched: price has not reached the zone.
- Approaching: price is getting close.
- Inside: price is testing the zone now.
- Respected: price tested the zone and reacted correctly.
- Failed: price closed through or disrespected the zone.

If a zone fails, discard it and watch the next valid zone.

If a zone is respected, drop to the entry timeframe and wait for sweep + BOS.

## 16. Buy Setup Checklist

A buy setup should follow this story:

1. Market structure is bullish, or price is reacting from valid HTF demand.
2. Price has pulled back into discount or demand.
3. The buy area is not in the middle of the range.
4. Sell-side liquidity exists below a low.
5. Price sweeps sell-side liquidity.
6. Price rejects after the sweep.
7. Price breaks a meaningful swing high.
8. The break has displacement.
9. A clear demand zone caused the shift.
10. The demand zone is fresh or lightly tested.
11. Entry is planned at the upper edge of demand.
12. Stop is beyond the demand zone or sweep low.
13. Target has at least 3R of room.
14. The target is a weak high, buy-side liquidity, opposing supply, or fixed 3R.
15. No high-impact news or conflicting context makes the setup unsafe.

If any major step is missing, the setup is not verified.

## 17. Sell Setup Checklist

A sell setup should follow this story:

1. Market structure is bearish, or price is reacting from valid HTF supply.
2. Price has pulled back into premium or supply.
3. The sell area is not in the middle of the range.
4. Buy-side liquidity exists above a high.
5. Price sweeps buy-side liquidity.
6. Price rejects after the sweep.
7. Price breaks a meaningful swing low.
8. The break has displacement.
9. A clear supply zone caused the shift.
10. The supply zone is fresh or lightly tested.
11. Entry is planned at the lower edge of supply.
12. Stop is beyond the supply zone or sweep high.
13. Target has at least 3R of room.
14. The target is a weak low, sell-side liquidity, opposing demand, or fixed 3R.
15. No high-impact news or conflicting context makes the setup unsafe.

If any major step is missing, the setup is not verified.

## 18. Entry Execution

The entry is not the sweep.

The entry is not the BOS candle.

The entry is the pullback into the zone that caused the shift.

### Buy Entry

For buys, mark demand and enter at the upper edge of demand when price returns downward.

Why upper edge? Because price may react from the top of demand and never reach the bottom.

### Sell Entry

For sells, mark supply and enter at the lower edge of supply when price returns upward.

Why lower edge? Because price may react from the bottom of supply and never reach the top.

### Avoid Late Entries

Do not chase after price has already reacted strongly away from the zone.

Do not enter deep inside the zone after the original entry area has already been missed unless there is a new valid lower-timeframe setup.

## 19. Stop Loss

For learning and backtesting, the stop usually goes beyond the reason for the trade.

### Buy Stop

Place stop beyond:

- Demand zone low.
- Sweep low.
- Strong low.

Use a small buffer where appropriate.

### Sell Stop

Place stop beyond:

- Supply zone high.
- Sweep high.
- Strong high.

Use a small buffer where appropriate.

### Stop Rule

If the stop must be too large and the trade no longer offers clean 3R, skip it.

## 20. Take Profit

The strategy does not treat 1R as the goal.

The normal minimum target is 3R.

Possible targets:

- Fixed 3R.
- Weak high for buys.
- Weak low for sells.
- Opposing supply for buys.
- Opposing demand for sells.
- Range high / range low.
- Buy-side or sell-side liquidity.

If the nearest clean target only gives 1R or 2R, the trade is not worth taking under this model.

If the next weak structure gives 4R, 5R, or more, that can be a valid target if the chart context supports it.

## 21. Trade Management

The simplified Trading Geek plan is usually set-and-forget.

Default:

- No trailing stop.
- No partial profits.
- No break-even as the main goal.
- Full TP or full SL.

Moving to break-even, partial profits, or trailing can be tested as separate management plans, but they should not be mixed into the core strategy unless proven.

## 22. Session Timing

Better setups usually happen during active sessions.

Preferred sessions:

- London.
- New York.
- London/New York overlap.

Be cautious during:

- Very low-liquidity hours.
- Market close/open spread expansion.
- Dead Asian session unless trading a pair active in that session.
- Major news windows.

Session timing is context, not a complete signal.

## 23. Fundamentals And News

Technical setup is the main focus, but fundamentals can affect caution.

Examples:

- If USD is fundamentally strong, be cautious selling USD.
- If USD is fundamentally weak, be cautious buying USD.
- If central bank news is near, avoid forcing entries.
- If CPI, NFP, FOMC, interest-rate decisions, or major speeches are near, technical setups can fail quickly.

Fundamentals should not replace the chart, but they can reduce confidence or cause a skip.

## 24. No-Trade Conditions

Do not trade when:

- Structure is unclear.
- Price is in the middle of the range.
- No liquidity was swept.
- Sweep is tiny or unclear.
- BOS is weak or internal noise.
- There is no clean supply/demand zone.
- Zone is too wide.
- Zone is already over-mitigated.
- Price compresses slowly into the zone.
- Price already reacted and entry is late.
- Target does not offer at least 3R.
- HTF and LTF stories conflict.
- Fundamentals strongly conflict.
- High-impact news is too close.
- Multiple signals contradict each other.
- You are forcing a trade because you want action.

## 25. Compression Into A Zone

Compression means price slowly crawls into a zone with many small candles and little rejection.

This can be dangerous because it often means the zone is being consumed.

A clean reaction is better than slow compression.

If price compresses into supply, the supply may fail.

If price compresses into demand, the demand may fail.

Compression does not automatically invalidate every setup, but it reduces quality.

## 26. Freshness

Fresh zones are usually better.

Zone quality decreases when:

- Price has already tapped the zone many times.
- The zone was deeply mitigated.
- The reaction already happened.
- Too many candles passed after BOS.
- The setup is stale.

If price returns too late, wait for fresh structure.

## 27. Common Mistakes

Avoid these mistakes:

- Entering only because price swept liquidity.
- Entering only because price touched supply or demand.
- Buying in premium.
- Selling in discount.
- Trading the middle of the range.
- Calling every candle an order block.
- Calling every imbalance an entry.
- Entering before BOS.
- Entering against the higher-timeframe story.
- Using M5 or M1 to invent trades against HTF direction.
- Chasing after price has already left the zone.
- Taking 1R trades when the model requires 3R.
- Moving stop or taking partials without a tested plan.
- Ignoring news.
- Overtrading weak pairs or dead sessions.

## 28. Setup Quality Grades

### A-Grade Setup

An A-grade setup has:

- Clear direction.
- Clean active range.
- Correct premium/discount location.
- Obvious liquidity sweep.
- Meaningful BOS / Market Shift.
- Strong displacement.
- Compact supply/demand zone.
- Fresh or lightly tested zone.
- Clean 3R+ target room.
- No major conflict from HTF or news.

### B-Grade Setup

A B-grade setup has most of the story but one or two weaknesses:

- Sweep is not very obvious.
- BOS is acceptable but not powerful.
- Zone is slightly wide.
- Target room is exactly 3R but not more.
- Session is not ideal.

These may be watchlist or reduced-confidence setups.

### C-Grade Setup

A C-grade setup is weak:

- Middle of range.
- Weak structure.
- No clear sweep.
- Weak BOS.
- Choppy zone.
- Late entry.
- Poor target room.

Avoid or use only for study.

## 29. Pair Selection And Value Context

The strategy can identify valid technical setups across many pairs, but not all pairs perform equally.

In the current project validation, the cleaner pair group was:

- `GBP_USD`
- `GBP_JPY`
- `USD_CAD`
- `USD_JPY`
- `XAU_USD`

Pairs that required caution from testing:

- `AUD_USD`
- `BTC_USD`
- `EUR_USD`
- `NZD_USD`

Important: a low-value pair label does not mean every setup is invalid. It means the pair showed weaker tested edge and should be treated with more caution.

The technical setup still needs to be judged by the same checklist.

## 30. Full Buy Example

Imagine price is bullish on M15.

1. Price makes higher highs and higher lows.
2. A strong low caused the latest bullish BOS.
3. Price pulls back into discount.
4. There is sell-side liquidity below a clean low.
5. Price sweeps below that low.
6. Price rejects and breaks a meaningful swing high.
7. The bullish break leaves a compact demand base.
8. That demand base caused the shift.
9. Entry is marked at the upper edge of demand.
10. Stop goes below demand and the sweep low.
11. Target is the weak high above or fixed 3R.

This is a valid buy story.

## 31. Full Sell Example

Imagine price is bearish on M15.

1. Price makes lower highs and lower lows.
2. A strong high caused the latest bearish BOS.
3. Price pulls back into premium.
4. There is buy-side liquidity above a clean high.
5. Price sweeps above that high.
6. Price rejects and breaks a meaningful swing low.
7. The bearish break leaves a compact supply base.
8. That supply base caused the shift.
9. Entry is marked at the lower edge of supply.
10. Stop goes above supply and the sweep high.
11. Target is the weak low below or fixed 3R.

This is a valid sell story.

## 32. Daily Practice Routine

Use this routine to practice.

1. Open the higher timeframe.
2. Mark the current direction.
3. Mark active range high and low.
4. Mark premium, discount, and equilibrium.
5. Mark obvious liquidity.
6. Mark supply and demand zones.
7. Decide where price needs to come before any trade is interesting.
8. Drop to the entry timeframe only when price is near the area.
9. Wait for sweep.
10. Wait for BOS / Market Shift.
11. Mark the zone that caused the shift.
12. Check 3R target room.
13. Decide whether the setup is A-grade, B-grade, weak, or no-trade.
14. Record the result.

## 33. Short Form Checklist

Before a trade, ask:

1. What is the direction?
2. What is the active range?
3. Are we in premium or discount?
4. Where is liquidity?
5. Has liquidity been swept?
6. Was there a real Market Shift / BOS?
7. What zone caused the shift?
8. Is the zone fresh and compact?
9. Is the entry at the correct edge?
10. Is the stop beyond the zone/sweep?
11. Is there at least 3R to target?
12. Is the setup fresh, or stale?
13. Is there compression into the zone?
14. Is there news or fundamental conflict?
15. Is this truly a verified setup, or am I forcing it?

## 34. One-Line Summary

Find the active market story, wait for price to reach the right premium/discount area, let liquidity be swept, require a real structure shift, enter from the zone that caused the shift, and target at least 3R or the next weak structure while skipping unclear, middle-of-range, stale, or conflicted setups.

## 35. Final Warning

This guide is for education and chart study. It is not financial advice and it is not a guarantee of profit.

The strategy requires patience. Most charts are no-trade most of the time. The edge comes from waiting for the full story, not from forcing entries.
